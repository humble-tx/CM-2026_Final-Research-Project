




# Testing Observations

## Test Session 1 – First-person Prototype

<img width="4032" height="3024" alt="537e385e7b0f3ebd8242bd9202d2f9a4" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/197fa7fa-f456-4ecd-aa08-c6cd31044379" />

### What I observed:

Players were confused and thought the game was broken because they could not control anything.

### What surprised me:

Most players did not realise that they were experiencing Pong from the perspective of the ball.

### Changes made:

This led me to search for another way to communicate the identity of the ball, which later introduced sound and vibration feedback.

## Test Session 2 – PS5 Vibration Prototype

<img width="1815" height="1279" alt="d40c726a8fcebb31e9583e971f7922aa" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/5e73d5b5-7685-4774-90ba-dc2ca1196bfb" />

### What I observed:

After adding vibration feedback through the PS5 controller, players immediately understood that collisions were happening to them.

### What surprised me:

Players could recognise themselves as the ball without any instructions.

### Changes made:

Although the identity problem was solved, players quickly became bored. This encouraged me to redesign the gameplay structure.

## Test Session 3 – Narrative Interpretation

<img width="4032" height="3024" alt="74ef37f7668c5a4fb8ae0e148003bc5a" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/4fec708a-4ad1-4a13-9bf5-7a252eff5124" />

### What I observed:

Some players misunderstood the goal zones of Act 3 and tried to avoid them.

### What surprised me:

Several players suggested adding text, dialogue, or inner monologues to explain the story and guide the experience.

### Reflection:

Although this would make the game easier to understand, I decided not to add textual instructions.

Throughout the project, I wanted players to understand the ball through interaction rather than language. Using text would explain the meaning directly and reduce the ambiguity and discovery that I wanted to preserve.

### Changes made:

Instead of adding dialogue, I continued using non-verbal methods such as vibration, sound, and changes in scale to strengthen the narrative.

I introduced clearer visual progression by changing the size of the circular boundary after each score.

## Test Session 4 – Hardware Prototype

<img width="4032" height="3024" alt="69af28bd42adfa97aee4a6ef85ac18d3" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/b9c84adf-07e1-41d7-a404-4ec00c88dc85" />

### What I observed:

The IMU and electromagnet prototypes frequently failed because of unstable connections and power issues.

### What surprised me:

Even when the hardware did work, the timing of repeated collisions was difficult to reproduce physically.

### Changes made:

I switched from MPU9250 to MPU6050 and experimented with different battery and MOSFET configurations.

## Test Session 5 – Keyboard vs Ball Controller

<img width="4032" height="3024" alt="2d683f6845dd191346899772e394e13a" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/95de3109-dd09-42d5-bb4f-e1724ad4c4fc" />

### What I observed:

Players consistently achieved better scores using the keyboard.

### What surprised me:

Despite poorer performance, players preferred the ball controller because it felt more immersive and playful.

### Changes made:

This changed my understanding of the project. I realised that better efficiency does not necessarily create a better experience.
